Llullaillaco (Spanish pronunciation: [ʎuʎajˈʎako]) is a dormant stratovolcano at the border of Argentina (Salta Province) and Chile (Antofagasta Region). It lies in the Puna de Atacama, a region of tall volcanic peaks on a high plateau close to the Atacama Desert, one of the driest places in the world.

At 6,723 metres (22,057 ft), Llullaillaco is the second highest active volcano in the world after Ojos del Salado, also in the Andes. It is the seventh highest peak in the Andes mountain range.

Etymology

The name "Llullaillaco" comes from the Kunza language and means "hot water" or "treacherous waters", referring to the nearby hot springs and the dangerous conditions on the mountain.

Geography

The volcano stands at the border between Chile and Argentina. The summit can be reached from both countries, though the Argentine side is generally considered easier for climbing. The mountain is surrounded by smaller volcanic peaks and extensive lava flows from past eruptions.

Archaeological significance

Llullaillaco is famous for the discovery of the "Children of Llullaillaco" in 1999, three Inca child mummies found near the summit. These mummies, dating from around 1500 AD, are among the best-preserved mummies ever found. The children were likely sacrificed as part of the Inca ritual of capacocha.
